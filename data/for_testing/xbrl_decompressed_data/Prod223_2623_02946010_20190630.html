The trustees present their report and financial statements for the year ended 30 June 2019.

The accounts have been prepared in accordance with the accounting policies set out in note 1 to the accounts and comply with the charity's governing document, the Companies Act 2006 and “Accounting and Reporting by Charities: Statement of Recommended Practice applicable to charities preparing their accounts in accordance with the Financial Reporting Standard applicable in the UK and Republic of Ireland (FRS 102)” (as amended for accounting periods commencing from 1 January 2016)

 

Objectives and activities

The objects of the Charity are the advancement of medical education and the relief of sickness, affliction and physical disability of the populations of developing and less favoured countries and communities, including communities within developed industrial countries. There have been no changes in the objects since the last annual report.

 

When planning the activities for the year, the Directors have considered the Charity Commission’s guidance on public benefit and are satisfied that the Charity’s activities met the principles laid out in that guidance. The Charity aims to benefit the public in the communities with which it works by raising funds and donations for conducting health education, policy research and humanitarian assistance programmes; and to support critical health programmes and pursue long term strategies leading to improvements in the health status of vulnerable children that can be sustained after Project HOPE UK’s work has been completed.

 

In the year to 30 June 2019, the Directors agreed that the Charity would devote its best efforts to the further development of its signature programme to transform the care and well-being of orphans and other vulnerable children in Munsieville, South Africa, aiming to create an influential “model of excellence” that can be adapted and replicated by impoverished communities worldwide.

 

In line with the Directors’ commitment to see Project HOPE UK become an organisation with a core specialism in the health and well-being of vulnerable children in less-developed countries, efforts have been made to build on the operational successes of recent years to develop a broad-based network of local groups and individuals who are being equipped and empowered to become agents of transformational change with the aim of affording all children in the community the opportunity to emerge into adulthood as healthy, productive citizens.

Grant making policy

Project HOPE UK voluntarily agrees to work with other organisations to deliver some activities on its behalf. It does not consider unsolicited funding requests but does, from time to time, provide grants or donations of medical supplies to external charitable organisations to undertake certain humanitarian activities that are pre-determined by the Board of directors and which are in line with the strategic objectives of Project HOPE UK.

Achievements and performance

The Charity has continued to concentrate on this programme, which is now a little over eight years into a ten-year commitment. The purpose of the programme is to seek lasting and sustainable solutions to the many drivers of poor health and child mortality in townships in Southern Africa, based on broad-based community engagement. The principal location for this work is Munsieville township in Gauteng Province.

In the first two years of implementing the programme, our local team identified six key changes that would be needed to transform Munsieville from a place of danger and hostility to the development of vulnerable children, to become a sustainable, child-friendly community in which the interests of children are prioritised. Significant progress on these six themes would equip the community to continue to effect changes to negative behaviours affecting children, whilst honouring the rich and diverse cultures of the township. The six key changes identified were:

 

  1. Rejecting poor quality pre-school care and insisting on professional early childhood development for all infants.

  2. Householders accepting personal responsibility for the safety of children within the home, eradicating the risk of fire, poisoning and other home-based hazards.

  3. Creating a culture of “zero-tolerance” towards child abuse, replacing acceptance of abuse with a new culture of reporting and accountability.

  4. Caregivers refusing to accept malnutrition in their children and embracing their role as pro-active agents in food security.

  5. Parents/caregivers see themselves as champions of the health of the children in their care and the prime activists in accessing health services for them.

  6. Rejection of dependency and a “hand-out” culture, replaced by a new belief in personal, family and community wealth creation based on the assets of the township.

 

Good progress has been made in respect of points 1, 2, 3 and 4. To this end, most of the human and financial resources over the period covered by this report have been devoted to the further development of point 4 and the remaining two priorities.

 

Addressing Child Malnutrition. The “You Grow: They Grow” initiative which is in its 3rd year has moved almost completely to Munsieville in terms of management, training and community engagement. In excess of 300 high-yield, drought-resistant food gardens, constructed according to keyhole gardening principles, have been installed to date, providing nutrient-rich fresh vegetables for 12 months in the year benefitting in excess of 1,500 children. From the dedicated Child Nutrition Centre donated by the Norton Rose Fulbright Foundation regular workshops on nutrition are offered which attracted many external stakeholders as well as the Department of Health. In June 2018 the Deutsche Bank Foundation in Sandton donated a number of new gardening tools and funded Permaculture Training delivered by the NGO ‘Food and Trees for Africa’. This part of the programme is already making great steps towards sustainability as garden groups were formed and community members support each other. A recent survey conducted by us shows an increase of variety of vegetables consumed in households, compared with the baseline from 2015 as well as a much more positive attitude towards a healthier diet. Excess vegetables are being sold on the market and contribute to income generation.

 

Access to Healthcare Services. After the opening of Hope Park in the heart of the informal settlement, a number of 7-12 stakeholders started operating from those premises. The principal agreement is that they are not being asked for rent payments and in return offer their services to the community for free. Services include but are not limited to HIV testing and counselling, Alcohol and Substance abuse counselling and referrals, Medical Male Circumcision, Clinical Nutrition, Social Services, Oral Health, Family Planning and reproductive Health. Child vaccinations will be delivered by the Department of Health once the required formal permission is granted on district level and a reliable electricity connection is established.

 

The official opening of Hope Park took place in September 2017 with the Mogale City municipality present as well as relevant stakeholders, the Department of Health and representatives of the Project HOPE UK Board of Directors. Due to the high demand services started before the opening date. In May 2018 we were granted permission by the municipality to get our own electricity connection at Hope Park.

 

Botshabelo Child Crisis Line. This 24-hour, toll-free support line is dedicated to the children of Munsieville township and is operated by a team of volunteers, trained in “first response” and able to give immediate support and referral to over fifteen affiliated organisations offering professional services. It also greatly encourages community members to report cases personally.

Home Safety and Resilience. In common with informal settlements throughout South Africa, Munsieville township witnessed the death and maiming of children every year because of home-based incidents, particularly fires and ingestion of paraffin. Over the year we have continued with widespread community campaigning to raise awareness of unnecessary risks and to introduce simple, low-cost measures to reduce them and mitigate their impact. The campaign, first launched in 2012, has led to a dramatic reduction in the number of reported injuries from hazards in the home and now, for several years in succession, no child fatalities because of fire were recorded.

 

Fhulofhelo Children’s Centre and No Child Left Behind. Fhulofhelo Centre is a facility within Hope Park and was constructed in 2016. Young children who are excluded from mainstream education due to their non-South African status come to receive basic education to prepare them for schools, whist efforts are made to obtain the documents needed to access state school services. Through the year, 34 children have been enrolled in the No Child Left Behind project, with active participation of parents. The project has proved highly successful in getting children into the school system, with 28 children from the current intake being accepted into primary school at the beginning of the next school year. With the facility being located within Hope Park, an added benefit for the children is immediate access to a range of health services which would previously have been out of reach. This part of the programme is very time consuming but has a nearly 100% success rate. It requires the special expertise of our senior case worker.

 

Fundraising

 

The Charity has been sustained through the generous and committed support of a small number of individuals, Trustees and staff members and is undertaking strenuous efforts to raise higher levels of unrestricted funding as well as project funding to cover vital aspects of the charity’s operations in South Africa.

The Property Race Day approved our application for a children’s sports and performance arena next to Hope Park which will complete the set up.

The Charity has moved to establish a strong fundraising function in South Africa and expects to raise a large proportion of the funds needed to cover vital services delivered in the country in future years. Liz Westby-Nunn from South Africa was appointed as a fundraising consultant in January 2018. Her brief was to explore funding opportunities and inform the Board on possible options. This was an essential step towards transitioning fundraising for South Africa into the country and many important contacts were made.

Financial review

The Trustees recognise the risks associated with operating vital services to beneficiaries whilst having very low levels of reserves, and have regular Board-level discussions to ensure that all commitments are met in the short-term, whilst urgent action is taken to secure necessary funding on a sustainable basis for the future. They continue to exercise close control of expenditure and where possible reduce the administrative costs of the Charity.

 

Voluntary help and donations in kind

 

All members of the Board gave their time freely and without charge.

 

We acknowledge and greatly appreciate the kind, ongoing support of GlaxoSmithKline PLC, for providing rent-free office accommodation, IT services and many other services to help the Charity keep running costs to a minimum. The Board is also indebted to students of East Tennessee State University, USA, Brunel University Uxbridge, University of the Witwatersrand, Johannesburg and Monash University South Africa for the valuable contributions they have made in the areas of public and community health and impact measurement.

Reserves

 

Project Hope UK uses its resources very efficiently and effectively. This is evidenced by

 

1.     Very low frictional costs - of each pound raised 94% is spent on direct project activities.

 

2.     Delivering what is promised - 100% record of completing projects started even those that require multiple year implementation.

 

3.    Putting money raised to work - Project Hope UK puts cash to work and runs with low reserves as cash sitting in our bank account is not helping to address the needs our work has identified. A key reason why this is possible and a much lower risk than for other charities is the unusually low overhead and fixed cost structure that we have which also helps us deliver 1 above. Higher levels of reserves can be carried if this is a requirement of donors.

 

The Trustees recognise that the income of the Charity is subject to fluctuation and is committed to building reserves in coming years to ensure services to beneficiaries are maintained. In the past year, the Charity has seen a temporary decrease of £738 in its unrestricted reserves, ending the year with a balance of £8,544 and is working to restore higher levels of reserves for the future.

 

Risk management

 

The Trustees operate a rolling risk assessment programme, with quarterly review, covering the major risks to which the Charity is exposed, including those relating to the operations, employees, volunteers and beneficiaries of the charity and, in the current circumstances, the finances of the Charity and the low levels of free reserves it carries. They are satisfied that systems are in place to mitigate exposure to major risks in the UK and in its centres of activity overseas.

 

Plans for future periods

 

The focus of the charity over the coming year will remain to be strengthening the Thoughtful Path programme, in particular:

 

  1. Further development of the services offered from the new Hope Park Children’s Health Campus, primarily through external service providers.

  2. Expanding the reach and impact of the Child Nutrition Centre and the You Grow: They Grow vegetable garden project.

  3. Further work to promote volunteering and engagement of community members in activities to improve outcomes for vulnerable children.

  4. Develop and plan the transition period to local fundraising and ownership including a strong South African Board of Directors.

Accounting policies
Charity information

Project Hope, United Kingdom is a private company limited by guarantee incorporated in England and Wales. The registered office is 66 Lincoln's Inn Fields, London, WC2A 3LH.

1.1
Accounting convention

The accounts have been prepared in accordance with the charity's governing document, the Companies Act 2006 and “Accounting and Reporting by Charities: Statement of Recommended Practice applicable to charities preparing their accounts in accordance with the Financial Reporting Standard applicable in the UK and Republic of Ireland (FRS 102)” (as amended for accounting periods commencing from 1 January 2016). The charity is a Public Benefit Entity as defined by FRS 102.

 

The charity has taken advantage of the provisions in the SORP for charities applying FRS 102 Update Bulletin 1 not to prepare a Statement of Cash Flows.

The financial statements are prepared in sterling, which is the functional currency of the charity. Monetary amounts in these financial statements are rounded to the nearest £.

The financial statements have been prepared under the historical cost convention. The principal accounting policies adopted are set out below.

1.2
Going concern

The charity is reliant upon the continued support of its donors to fund its ongoing charitable work. As indicated in the trustees' report, the trustees are taking urgent action to adjust expenditure and increase funding to ensure that the charity is able to continue as a going concern.

1.3
Charitable funds
Unrestricted funds are available for use at the discretion of the trustees in furtherance of their charitable objectives unless the funds have been designated for other purposes.

Restricted funds are subject to specific conditions by donors as to how they may be used. The purposes and uses of the restricted funds are set out in the notes to the financial statements.

1.4
Incoming resources
Income is recognised when the charity is legally entitled to it after any performance conditions have been met, the amounts can be measured reliably, and it is probable that income will be received.
Cash donations are recognised on receipt. Other donations are recognised once the charity has been notified of the donation, unless performance conditions require deferral of the amount. Income tax recoverable in relation to donations received under Gift Aid or deeds of covenant is recognised at the time of the donation.
Assets for distribution are recognised only when distributed. Assets given for use by the charity are recognised when receivable. Stocks of undistributed donated goods are not valued for balance sheet purposes.

1.5
Resources expended

All expenditure is accounted for gross, and when incurred. 1.6
Cash and cash equivalents

Cash and cash equivalents include cash in hand, deposits held at call with banks, other short-term liquid investments with original maturities of three months or less, and bank overdrafts. Bank overdrafts are shown within borrowings in current liabilities.

1.7
Financial instruments

The charity has elected to apply the provisions of Section 11 ‘Basic Financial Instruments’ and Section 12 ‘Other Financial Instruments Issues’ of FRS 102 to all of its financial instruments.

 

Financial instruments are recognised in the charity's balance sheet when the charity becomes party to the contractual provisions of the instrument.

 

Financial assets and liabilities are offset, with the net amounts presented in the financial statements, when there is a legally enforceable right to set off the recognised amounts and there is an intention to settle on a net basis or to realise the asset and settle the liability simultaneously.

Basic financial assets

Basic financial assets, which include debtors and cash and bank balances, are initially measured at transaction price including transaction costs and are carried at cost unless the arrangement constitutes a financing transaction, where the transaction is measured at the present value of the future receipts discounted at a market rate of interest. Financial assets classified as receivable within one year are not amortised.

Basic financial liabilities

Basic financial liabilities, including creditors and bank loans are initially recognised at transaction price unless the arrangement constitutes a financing transaction. Financial liabilities classified as payable within one year are not amortised.

 

Debt instruments are subsequently carried at amortised cost, using the effective interest rate method.

 

Derecognition of financial liabilities

Financial liabilities are derecognised when the charity’s contractual obligations expire or are discharged or cancelled.

1.8
Employee benefits

The cost of any unused holiday entitlement is recognised in the period in which the employee’s services are received.

 

Termination benefits are recognised immediately as an expense when the charity is demonstrably committed to terminate the employment of an employee or to provide termination benefits.
