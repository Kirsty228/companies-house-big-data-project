STRATEGIC REPORT
FOR THE YEAR ENDED 30 JUNE 2019
- 1 -

The directors present the strategic report for the year ended 30 June 2019.

Business review

The company posted a pre-tax profit of £63K (2018 - £170k) on a turnover of £27M, up from £23M in 2018. Tonnage was up by 9% (2018 - 18%) and the average price per tonne increased 5% (2018 - 10%) during the course of the year which reflected the input price rise of steel of 5% (2018 - 17%). The margin on materials increased during the year by 7% largely due to the new products and processes within sectors referred to in last year’s report.

In common with other sectors of manufacturing industry, according to published indices, the company has battled through a year of fluctuating demand which has not followed previous trends. Our business development teams have worked consistently with customers to develop new products and enhance existing ones both to consolidate relationships and generate margin. These efforts are continuing into fiscal 2020.

Our response to the Covid-19 crisis, very much in line with the Prime Minister’s mantra that ‘we must all pull together’, is to focus on the safety and welfare of our employees and those of our suppliers visiting our site; and looking after our customers.

The company has continued to invest in the future. Its investment in Hi-Tech Resourcing Limited (HTR) now stands at £932K (£401K). As a commitment to what is effectively a new business, it covers a period in which HTR has:

  • moved to new and more suitable premises;

  • developed a significant number of new, high margin products;

  • significantly improved its production processes

  • invested consistently in its workforce.

HTR reported a profit of £83K in the 6 months to June (the last set of accounts). Fiscal 2020 has started promisingly with orders consistently hitting target above the required break-even level and the demand for new products continuing to increase. HTR’s 4 strong design team typically has 3 months’ work in the pipeline. Most recently designs have been approved for environmentally positive products in the automotive and water resource management sectors. The Directors expect the benefits of these products will be seen in modest increases in turnover and profitability in fiscal 2020 but the full impact will show in fiscal 2021.

Exports, all within the EU, still form an important part of the Company’s activities at 17% of turnover (2018: 18%). The Directors are aware of the threat posed by Brexit and are working with customers to mitigate some of the obvious risks posed by the transition. In common with other businesses in our sector we view the election result in a positive light as it should give us further clarity on how the UK proposes to move forward.

As to the future, the Company believes it will bring more opportunities from the proposed infrastructure spending outlined in the run-up to the general election in December 2019. We have put in place 2 new teams to look at more value-added products for our laser and the computer software development required to take full advantage of the findings. These teams are being run in conjunction with Liverpool John Moores University including 2 graduates and senior academic support.

Customer Service and Quality levels remain high and this is testament to the dedication and expertise of our close-knit, back office team and production staff. In addition we are continuing to invest in training and new computer systems to support them.

Lastly, we thank our financiers and suppliers for their continuing support.

The Directors and Management Team are conscious of the risks in today's global market, the uncertainties of Brexit, the impact of the international response to the coronavirus and the fluctuations in both commodity prices and demand which affect the steel industry. We have audited the financial statements of Hi-Tech Steel Services Limited (the 'company') for the year ended 30 June 2019 which comprise the profit and loss account, the statement of comprehensive income, the balance sheet, the statement of changes in equity, the statement of cash flows and notes to the financial statements, including a summary of significant accounting policies. In our opinion, except for the effects of the matter described in the Basis for Qualified Opinion paragraph, the financial statements:

  •     give a true and fair view of the state of the company's affairs as at 30 June 2019 and of its profit for the year then ended;

  •     have been properly prepared in accordance with United Kingdom Generally Accepted Accounting Practice; and

  •     have been prepared in accordance with the requirements of the Companies Act 2006.

Basis for qualified opinion

As discussed in Note 3 the company has assets of £2,204,000 committed to an associated company, Hi Tech Resourcing Limited. The directors are confident that the future trading of this entity will be sufficient to support the carrying values of all of these assets and accordingly have not recorded any impairment. We disagree with this treatment and consider that given the low and volatile levels of profitability of Hi Tech Resourcing Limited the requirements of FRS 102 to carry such financial instruments at their market value should lead to an impairment.

 

We conducted our audit in accordance with International Standards on Auditing (UK) (ISAs (UK)) and applicable law. Our responsibilities under those standards are further described in the Auditor's responsibilities for the audit of the financial statements section of our report. We are independent of the company in accordance with the ethical requirements that are relevant to our audit of the financial statements in the UK, including the FRC’s Ethical Standard, and we have fulfilled our other ethical responsibilities in accordance with these requirements. We believe that the audit evidence we have obtained is sufficient and appropriate to provide a basis for our opinion.

Material Uncertainty Related to Going Concern

We draw attention to Note 2 in the financial statements, which describes the financial position of the company and indicates that company experienced a cash outflow in the year due to the investments made in the associated company Hi Tech Resourcing Limited. Given the very significant present and potential impact on the customer base of the company by the uncertainty of Britain’s exit from the European Union and the global response to the coronavirus, we consider that there is a material uncertainty as to whether the company has adequate financial reserves to meet its obligations as they fall due for the foreseeable future. The directors have disclosed these circumstances in Note 2 and therefore our opinion has not been modified in these respects.

1.1
Accounting convention

These financial statements have been prepared in accordance with FRS 102 “The Financial Reporting Standard applicable in the UK and Republic of Ireland” (“FRS 102”) and the requirements of the Companies Act 2006.

The financial statements are prepared in sterling, which is the functional currency of the company. Monetary amounts in these financial statements are rounded to the nearest £.

The financial statements have been prepared under the historical cost convention, modified to include certain financial instruments being carried at fair value. The principal accounting policies adopted are set out below.

1.2
Going concern

These financial statements are prepared on the going concern basis. The directors have a reasonable expectation that the company will continue in operational existence for the foreseeable future. However, the directors are aware of certain material uncertainties which may cause doubt on the company's ability to continue as a going concern. See further details in Note 2.

1.3
Turnover

Turnover is recognised at the fair value of the consideration received or receivable for goods and services provided in the normal course of business, and is shown net of VAT and other sales related taxes. The fair value of consideration takes into account trade discounts, settlement discounts and volume rebates.

 

Revenue from the sale of goods is recognised when the significant risks and rewards of ownership of the goods have passed to the buyer (usually on dispatch of the goods), the amount of revenue can be measured reliably, it is probable that the economic benefits associated with the transaction will flow to the entity and the costs incurred or to be incurred in respect of the transaction can be measured reliably.

1.4
Research and development expenditure

Research expenditure is written off against profits in the year in which it is incurred. Identifiable development expenditure is capitalised to the extent that the technical, commercial and financial feasibility can be demonstrated.

1.5
Intangible fixed assets - goodwill

Goodwill is the difference between amounts paid on the acquisition of a business and the fair value of the identifiable assets and liabilities. It is amortised to the profit and loss account over it's useful estimated economic life.

 

Where negative goodwill is created, in situations where the fair value of identifiable assets and liabilities are greater than the amounts paid on acquisition of a business, it is amortised to the profit and loss account by reference to the period over which non-monetary assets are recovered.

 

Amortisation is recognised so as to write off the cost or valuation of assets less their residual values over their useful lives on the following bases:

 

Goodwill - 7% straight line

Negative goodwill - 7% straight line or upon disposal of the related tangible fixed assets

1.7
Fixed asset investments

Investments in unlisted company shares, whose market value the directors consider can be reliably determined, are remeasured to market value at each balance sheet date. Gains and losses on remeasurement are recognised in other comprehensive income for the period. Any investments which the directors consider impossible to determine market value reliably are stated at historical cost less impairment.

A subsidiary is an entity controlled by the company. Control is the power to govern the financial and operating policies of the entity so as to obtain benefits from its activities.

Trade debtors, loans and other receivables that have fixed or determinable payments that are not quoted in an active market are classified as 'loans and receivables'. Loans and receivables are measured at amortised cost using the effective interest method, less any impairment.

 

Interest is recognised by applying the effective interest rate, except for short-term receivables when the recognition of interest would be immaterial. The effective interest method is a method of calculating the amortised cost of a debt instrument and of allocating the interest income over the relevant period. The effective interest rate is the rate that exactly discounts estimated future cash receipts through the expected life of the debt instrument to the net carrying amount on initial recognition.

1.8
Impairment of fixed assets

At each reporting period end date, the company reviews the carrying amounts of its tangible and intangible assets to determine whether there is any indication that those assets have suffered an impairment loss. If any such indication exists, the recoverable amount of the asset is estimated in order to determine the extent of the impairment loss (if any). Where it is not possible to estimate the recoverable amount of an individual asset, the company estimates the recoverable amount of the cash-generating unit to which the asset belongs.

1.9
Stocks

Stocks are stated at the lower of cost and estimated selling price less costs to complete and sell. Cost comprises direct materials and, where applicable, direct labour costs and those overheads that have been incurred in bringing the stocks to their present location and condition.

At each reporting date, an assessment is made for impairment. Any excess of the carrying amount of stocks over its estimated selling price less costs to complete and sell is recognised as an impairment loss in profit or loss. Reversals of impairment losses are also recognised in profit or loss.

1.12
Taxation

The tax expense represents the sum of the tax currently payable and deferred tax.

Current tax

The tax currently payable is based on taxable profit for the year. Taxable profit differs from net profit as reported in the profit and loss account because it excludes items of income or expense that are taxable or deductible in other years and it further excludes items that are never taxable or deductible. The company’s liability for current tax is calculated using tax rates that have been enacted or substantively enacted by the reporting end date.

Deferred tax

Deferred tax liabilities are generally recognised for all timing differences and deferred tax assets are recognised to the extent that it is probable that they will be recovered against the reversal of deferred tax liabilities or other future taxable profits. Such assets and liabilities are not recognised if the timing difference arises from goodwill or from the initial recognition of other assets and liabilities in a transaction that affects neither the tax profit nor the accounting profit.

 

The carrying amount of deferred tax assets is reviewed at each reporting end date and reduced to the extent that it is no longer probable that sufficient taxable profits will be available to allow all or part of the asset to be recovered. Deferred tax is calculated at the tax rates that are expected to apply in the period when the liability is settled or the asset is realised. Deferred tax is charged or credited in the profit and loss account, except when it relates to items charged or credited directly to equity, in which case the deferred tax is also dealt with in equity. Deferred tax assets and liabilities are offset when the company has a legally enforceable right to offset current tax assets and liabilities and the deferred tax assets and liabilities relate to taxes levied by the same tax authority.

1.13

Payments to defined contribution retirement benefit schemes are charged as an expense as they fall due.

1.14
Leases

Leases are classified as finance leases whenever the terms of the lease transfer substantially all the risks and rewards of ownership to the lessees. All other leases are classified as operating leases.

 

Assets held under finance leases are recognised as assets at the lower of the assets fair value at the date of inception and the present value of the minimum lease payments. The related liability is included in the balance sheet as a finance lease obligation. Lease payments are treated as consisting of capital and interest elements. The interest is charged to profit or loss so as to produce a constant periodic rate of interest on the remaining balance of the liability.

Rentals payable under operating leases, including any lease incentives received, are charged to profit or loss on a straight line basis over the term of the relevant lease except where another more systematic basis is more representative of the time pattern in which economic benefits from the leases asset are consumed.

1.15
Foreign exchange

Transactions in currencies other than pounds sterling are recorded at the rates of exchange prevailing at the dates of the transactions. At each reporting end date, monetary assets and liabilities that are denominated in foreign currencies are retranslated at the rates prevailing on the reporting end date. Gains and losses arising on translation are included in the profit and loss account for the period.

Going Concern

The company was profitable and generated operating cash of £1,708,796 in the year. However the funding provided to Hi-Tech Resourcing Limited to further the strategic objectives of the company (see Note 3) meant that cash flow for the year was negative.

 

In the period since the year end the company has continued to provide strategic funding to Hi-Tech Resourcing Limited during this key stage in its development, while dealing with a challenging trading environment. The current strong order book of the company (and the increasing sales and reduced monthly working capital requirements of Hi-Tech Resourcing Limited) give the directors comfort that the company will be able to meet its obligations as they fall due, particularly given its strong trading relationships

.

Economies throughout the world are currently experiencing extreme stress due to the spread of Coronavirus and the associated regulations and restrictions. This stress will lead to an uncertain level of orders from customers being delayed or cancelled and possible changes in credit limits from suppliers. The UK Government has stated that it will do ‘whatever it takes’ to ensure jobs are protected but the process of providing this government assistance is uncertain and may be delayed. Dependent on the scale of any such circumstances, it is not certain that that the facilities available to the company from its funders would be adequate to enable it to meet its obligations as they fall due

 

Conversely the crisis could promote an increasing reliance on home-grown manufacturing within the UK, benefiting the company’s trading prospects as questions are raised about the reliability of global supply chains. Having considered all these matters carefully, the directors believe that the company has adequate resources to continue trading for the foreseeable future and hence, consider that it is appropriate to prepare the financial statements on a going concern basis.
